# Scan-Decide-Receive (Two-Cone)

**Drill ID:** SX-DR-001
**Library status:** Inaugural drill — foundational scan-decide-receive sequence.
**Codex source:** v0.7.1 Part II — Capacities Framework, *Worked examples — drills as capacity combinations*.
**Library entry date:** 2026-05-25

---

## Codex tags

**Convictions instantiated**
- **Conviction 4 — First touch is the foundation skill.** The receiving touch is the unit of work; everything else builds on it.
- **Conviction 5 — Scanning is a habit, not a gift.** The drill makes scanning the *entry condition* of receiving — no scan, no clean touch toward space.
- **Conviction 3 — Decision-making is the ceiling.** What the player sees before the ball arrives drives where the touch goes; the touch becomes a chosen action, not a reaction.
- **Conviction 6 — Both feet, or half a player.** The drill alternates the receiving foot in every block; the weak foot is trained equally from the first session.
- **Conviction 28 — Ball mastery is irreplaceable.** Quality first-touch reps accumulate at scale; this is daily-touch work given perceptual and cognitive structure.
- **Conviction 30 — Cognitive load matters.** A scan + a decision are layered onto the touch; same touches, several times the developmental value.
- **Conviction 36 — Training must overdo gameplay complexity.** Even at baseline, the player is asked to do three things (scan, decide, touch) in one rep — more than most matches demand of a U12 in any single moment.

**Player Operating Principle reinforced**
- *Scan-decide-receive* — the canonical on-ball-receiving sequence in the Codex's Player Operating Principles.

**Capacities trained** (by family, primary / secondary distinguished)
- *Perceptual:* **primary** — visual scanning (peripheral, over-shoulder), spatial awareness. **secondary** — anticipation (timing the head turn to the ball's flight).
- *Cognitive:* **primary** — decision-making under constraint, selective attention. **secondary** — working memory (holding what was seen on the scan long enough to act on it), self-monitoring.
- *Motor:* **primary** — ball mastery (first-touch direction control, both feet). **secondary** — whole-body coordination (head turn while preparing the receiving foot), balance.
- *Communication:* *(not primary in baseline; introduced at Level 4 via caller variant)*.
- *Affective:* focus and concentration as multipliers; composure under the constraint of a constantly-arriving ball.
- *Adaptive:* *(seeded at Level 5 — surface, opponent, and call variants build adaptive capacity).*

## Metadata

- **Age band:** introducible from **9–12 Foundation**; central in **13–16 Development**; refined as a maintenance drill through **17–20 Specialisation** and beyond.
- **Level within band:** baseline beginner-to-intermediate at Level 1; reaches elite by Level 5.
- **Players:** 1 (solo, against a wall or rebounder) / 2 (player + server) / 3 (player + server + caller). Canonical group-size bucket: **1–3**.
- **Duration:** 8–15 min block within a session. Baseline 12 min: two 5-min foot blocks + a 2-min reflection.
- **Environment:** any flat surface ~8 m × 4 m — pitch, half-pitch, garden, indoor hall, park, playground.
- **Equipment:** **2 cones minimum, 1 ball.** With server: same setup. With a rebounder or wall (solo): 2 cones + 1 ball + wall. Optional 4 cones for the paired version (two flanking each player).
- **Primary objectives:** first touch, scanning, both feet, decision-making.
- **Difficulty:** 2 (baseline) → 5 (elite).
- **Individual focus:** every player in the drill — whether soloing against a wall or playing the role of server — is developing their own scan-decide-receive sequence. The drill is *for the individual*, never for a position; the server is not "passing practice for the passer," they are also building first-touch through the return touch on their reps. Develop the player, not the position (Conviction 14).

## Introduction

This is the first drill in the StunpreX library because it is the first habit StunpreX defends. Before the ball arrives, the player looks. Based on what they see, the first touch goes somewhere — toward open space, toward a chosen cone, away from imagined pressure. The drill makes *scanning the precondition of receiving*, not an optional extra a coach has to remind the player about.

Coaches who shout "scan!" all session are coaching against the design of their own drill. This drill is the opposite: it engineers the environment so that **the scan is the only way to know what the first touch should do**. Constraints, not commands (Conviction 13, made into the design method).

It is a *foundational* drill — modest by appearance, decisive by accumulation. A player who runs Level 1 for 10 minutes, three days a week, for six months arrives at Level 5 with a habit they will carry for the next fifteen years. That is the StunpreX bet on this drill.

## Setup

**Baseline (2 players, 4 cones):**

```
       Cone L (red)                                     Cone L (red)
          •                                                •
                                                              
                                                              
   [Player B] ←——————— 5 m ———————→ [Player A]
                                                              
                                                              
          •                                                •
       Cone R (blue)                                     Cone R (blue)
```

- **Centre line:** Player A and Player B face each other on a centre line, 5 m apart, each with the ball at their feet at the start of their pass.
- **Flanking cones:** each player has two cones — one ~2 m to their left, one ~2 m to their right, perpendicular to the centre line. Different colours help at Level 2 (e.g. red and blue).
- **Ball:** one ground ball between the two players.
- **Surface:** flat. If the only surface is uneven, that's fine — Conviction 22 (variability builds robustness) makes this a feature not a bug at Level 3+.

**Two-cone minimum version (solo or paired, 2 cones):**

If only 2 cones are available, place them flanking one player (the active receiver); the server (or wall) replaces the second set. The drill works equally well — the active player still has a left/right binary decision on every rep.

**Solo version (1 player, wall + 2 cones):** player stands 5 m from a wall. 2 cones flank them (left and right, 2 m apart). Player plays a firm pass into the wall, scans both shoulders during the ball's flight back, takes the first touch toward the chosen cone.

---

## Description (how it runs)

**One rep:**

1. Player A passes firmly along the ground to Player B.
2. **During the ball's flight,** Player B must visibly turn the head — once over the right shoulder, once over the left — looking at each of their flanking cones. (At Level 2+, the choice is constrained by a coach call; at Level 1, the player chooses freely based on what they see.)
3. Player B's **first touch** goes toward one of the two flanking cones — the choice is made *because of the scan*, not as a reflex.
4. Player B's **second touch** returns the ball cleanly to Player A.
5. Roles repeat: A plays to B again; or alternate so B plays to A on the next rep.

**One block:**

- **Block 1 (5 min):** receiving foot fixed — right. Players alternate the active role every 2 reps.
- **Rest (30 s):** breathing, mini-reflection ("which scan came easier?").
- **Block 2 (5 min):** receiving foot fixed — left. Same rotation.
- **Closing reflection (2 min):** see *Closing* below.

**End-of-rep condition:** clean return pass to A. A heavy first touch that escapes the player's control is a failed rep — *the failure is the data*; the player adjusts the next rep. No punishment, no shame; the next ball is already on its way.

**Reset / rotation:** continuous. A drill where the ball stops between reps wastes the perceptual training the drill exists to deliver.

## Coach guidance

**Look for** (the behaviours the drill is designed to develop):
- Visible head turn **before** the ball arrives — physical rotation, not just eye flicks. Without the rotation, the drill teaches nothing.
- Two scans per rep, not one. The drill is built on the both-shoulder scan; one-shoulder scanning is the most common early failure mode.
- The first touch *commits* — toward a cone, decisively. Half-touches that go straight back are the foot defaulting to the safe option; the drill is designed to break that default.
- Increasing comfort on the weak foot across the block. The weak-foot block is where the player learns what they actually have.

**Cues to give** (short, in-rep prompts — questions where possible):
- *"Scan first."*
- *"Both shoulders."*
- *"Where are your options?"*
- *"What did you see?"* (after a rep, not during)
- *"Which foot?"* (when the player is about to default to the strong one)

**Praise** (process, not outcome — Conviction 21):
- *"Good scan."*
- *"You looked twice."*
- *"Brave touch with the weak foot."*
- *"Good commit — even though it was heavy, the choice was clear."*

**Don't fix yet** (errors that resolve naturally with reps — intervene only after 5+ reps of the same):
- The touch direction itself in the first few reps. The scan is the primary behaviour; the touch will refine once the scan is reliable.
- The exact size of the head turn. As long as the scan is visible, leave the magnitude alone for the first session.
- Cone choice "errors." There is no wrong cone at Level 1 — the player is learning to decide, not to decide correctly.

## Progressions

Five levels. Each level changes one to two variables. The drill is the same drill across all five — what scales is the cognitive and perceptual load.

- **Level 1 — Baseline.** As described above. Two flanking cones, free choice of touch direction. The player scans and chooses; the coach reinforces the scan habit. Foot blocks of 5 min each (right, then left).

- **Level 2 — Coach call mid-flight.** Coach calls a cone colour (or "left" / "right") while the ball is in flight. Player must touch toward the called cone. *Variable added: constraint on the decision; selective attention layered on top of scanning.*

- **Level 3 — Third cone (working memory).** Add a third cone *behind* the receiver. Player must scan over both shoulders to see all three cones, then take the first touch toward the cone the coach calls — including the one behind. *Variable added: working memory (the over-shoulder cone was seen 1.5 s ago) and head-on-a-swivel scanning.*

- **Level 4 — Passive defender, 3 m back.** A second player stands 3 m behind the receiver as a passive presence (no tackle, but visibly there). The receiver must scan, register the defender's position, and touch *away from* the defender. *Variables added: real spatial pressure, decision-under-presence, the scan now has meaningful content.*

- **Level 5 — Active defender + small goal.** Defender starts 2 m away, active (allowed to win the ball). A small goal placed 8 m beyond the receiver's starting line. The receiver's first touch goes toward space, the second toward goal — within 4 seconds of the ball arriving. *Variables added: real pressure, time constraint, an outcome that matters. Training overdoes match complexity (Conviction 36).*

## Variations

- **Solo (home / garden / hotel-room corridor):** wall or rebounder replaces the server. 2 cones, 1 ball. Player passes into the wall, scans both shoulders during the return, first touch toward chosen cone. The most rep-dense version — useful for the *thousand-touches-a-day* commitment (Conviction 28).

- **Triangle (3 players, no extra equipment beyond 2 cones):** three players in a triangle ~5 m per side. Ball moves around the triangle. The receiving player scans both teammates *before* the ball arrives and takes the first touch toward whichever side they will play on next. Adds a real spatial reading — the cones become teammates.

- **Beach / uneven surface / weighted ball:** same drill, less predictable bounce. Develops adaptive capacity (Conviction 22). Useful as occasional disruption, not as the daily form — the surface variability tax should be a deliberate add, not the default.

- **Weak-foot-only block:** the entire 10–15 min runs on the weak foot. Done once a week as a dedicated weak-foot session, the drill closes the foot-asymmetry gap faster than mixed blocks (Conviction 6).

- **Caller variant (3 players, communication layer):** a third player stands behind the receiver and calls "left" / "right" / "back" during the ball's flight. Adds a listening + perception-coordination layer (Family 4 — Communication). The caller rotates in every block. Sub-skill: the *caller* is also developing — they are learning to read what the receiver can and cannot see, and to time the call to the ball's flight. Both roles are developmental.

## Watch points and redirect cues

Framed as questions the coach asks, not corrections the coach announces. The StunpreX coach archetype: constraints generate the behaviour; the coach asks the player to notice it.

- **Watch for:** Player scans only on the side the ball is coming from. **Redirect:** *"What's behind your other shoulder right now?"*
- **Watch for:** Player scans *after* the ball arrives, not before. **Redirect:** *"When does the scan happen — before or after the ball gets to your foot?"*
- **Watch for:** Player commits to a cone direction *before* scanning (scan becomes decorative). **Redirect:** *"Look first. Decide after. What did you actually see?"*
- **Watch for:** First touch goes straight back to the server rather than toward a cone (the safe-default touch). **Redirect:** *"Where did your touch go? Where was it supposed to go?"*
- **Watch for:** Weak-foot block — player rotates the body to use the strong foot. **Redirect:** *"Which foot did you receive with? Try it again — the weak one this time."*
- **Watch for:** Eye flicks instead of head turns (the player thinks they're scanning, but the head hasn't moved). **Redirect:** *"Can your teammate see your head turn? If not, the scan didn't really happen."*

## How to know it's working

Within the **first session:** the player produces visible two-shoulder scans on at least 50% of reps without coach prompting. The receiving touch begins to go *somewhere* — toward a cone, away from a defender — instead of always returning straight to the server.

Within **two weeks (4–6 sessions):** the scan is automatic on the strong foot. The weak foot still requires conscious effort but the touch is no longer abandoning. Player can describe what they saw on a given rep when asked.

Within **two months:** the scan happens reliably under Level 4 conditions (passive defender). Player begins to anticipate the defender's movement rather than just register their position. The drill begins to feel *easy* — that is the signal to move to Level 5 or to harder pairings.

Within **a season:** the scan-decide-receive sequence is no longer a drill; it is how the player receives in matches. Coaches who didn't run this drill comment that the player "always seems to know what's behind them" — that is what success looks like.

## Closing — five-minute reflection

In the StunpreX coach style: questions, not statements. The reflection is part of the drill; skipping it strips the self-coaching layer (Conviction 17 — self-assessment is a skill).

- *"What did you notice about your scanning today?"*
- *"Which foot felt easier? Why?"*
- *"What did you see on the scans that made you choose the cone you chose?"*
- *"How would this drill feel if there was a real opponent? Where would you scan first then?"*
- *"What would you change next time?"*

If the player can answer two of those five with specifics, the cognitive layer of the drill is doing its work.

## Parent-facing note

This is the first habit your player builds in the StunpreX system, and one of the most quietly important ones. Elite midfielders look around the pitch substantially more often than average players — not because they are gifted with vision, but because at some point in their development they were trained to make scanning a habit (Codex Conviction 5; Geir Jordet's research on elite midfielder visual exploration is the most-cited reference in the literature).

You don't need to run this drill — your player does. But you can help by **noticing the scan**, not the goal. If you watch a match and your only feedback afterward is "did you score?", you are missing what StunpreX is trying to build. A useful question after a session is: *"Did you remember to look around before the ball got to you?"* That single question, asked occasionally and without pressure, reinforces the habit the drill is teaching.

There is no urgency. There is no "behind." A 10-year-old who builds this habit over a season will carry it through every age band; a 14-year-old who builds it now is not late. Develop the player, not the result.

## Refusals — what this drill is NOT

- **Not position-specific.** This is not "first-touch training for midfielders." Every player on the pitch receives the ball; every player benefits from scanning before they do. The drill develops the player, not the position (Conviction 14).
- **Not early specialisation.** Running this drill three times a week alongside other sports, free play, and rest is healthy. Running it three times a week *instead of* other activities at age 10 is the kind of football-year-round specialisation the Codex rejects (Conviction 8).
- **Not a results drill.** Cone-touch accuracy is not the metric. The scan is the metric. A drill that selects the players who hit the cones is the wrong drill.
- **Not a punishment for missed reps.** Failed first touches are *data* (Conviction 25). The next ball is already on its way; the lesson is in the next rep, not in shame about the last one.
- **Not a substitute for free play.** Foundation-age players need protected free-play time (Conviction 7). Drill time is in addition to free play, not instead of.
- **Not a substitute for matches.** Scanning developed in this drill must be expressed in matches to consolidate. A player who only runs the drill and never plays games does not transfer the capacity (Codex Activity-Capacity Matrix — affective and adaptive layers require real stakes).
- **Not a parent-led pressure drill.** If the player begins to dread the drill or starts running it primarily to please a parent, the developmental value is already lost (Conviction 34 — pressure breaks the player you are trying to build).
