## Introduction

The Cone Sea trains what constraints make possible: creativity under pressure. The player dribbles continuously through a dense field of cones — the "sea" — while listening for the coach's gate call. When a gate is called, the player navigates toward it and exits cleanly. Then re-enters and continues.

The challenge is to do three things at once: keep the ball close, keep the head up, and stay alert to the next call. These three things do not compete — in a well-designed Cone Sea, they reinforce each other. Scanning helps you avoid cones. Avoiding cones keeps you mobile. Mobility is what lets you respond to the gate call.

This is not a relay race, and not a cone weave with a prescribed path. There is no correct route through the sea. The player invents their route on every rep — and that invention must account for what they can see, where they are, and where they need to go next. Ball mastery becomes the precondition for spatial navigation; spatial navigation becomes the precondition for responding to the call.

## Setup

Mark a **10m × 10m** square grid using four corner cones.

**The sea:** scatter 15–18 cones randomly inside the grid. Dense, but navigable — a player dribbling at match pace should be able to find a path in any direction, but no path should be straight or obvious. Re-randomise the layout between rounds and between sessions.

**The exit gates:** on three sides of the grid, mark one exit gate using two cones placed 1m apart at the midpoint of that side:
- **Gate A** — centre of the north side.
- **Gate B** — centre of the east side.
- **Gate C** — centre of the south side.

The west side is the entry/re-entry side — no gate there.

Label each gate clearly before starting: A/B/C, or colours (e.g. Red/Blue/Green). Use coloured cones if available; coloured bibs as gate markers also work. The player must locate and name all three gates before the drill begins.

The coach stands outside the grid on the west side, with sightlines to all three gates.

---

## Description (how it runs)

1. Player enters the sea from the west side, dribbling.
2. Player dribbles freely through the cone field — avoiding contact with any cone, keeping the ball within one foot's reach at all times, head up.
3. **Alternating-foot rule (baseline):** the player alternates the dribbling foot on each touch during the open-sea phase. Foot alternation is released only for the final 3–4 touches approaching and exiting the gate.
4. Coach calls one gate at irregular intervals — no call sooner than 8 seconds after the previous call, no longer than 20 seconds. Call is a single word: *"A!"* or *"Red!"* or *"B!"*.
5. On the call: player navigates to the called gate and exits cleanly — ball exits through the gate first, player follows. No stopping to plan the route; the navigation begins immediately.
6. Player re-enters through the same gate and continues dribbling in the sea.
7. Coach calls the next gate.
8. Round runs for 3 minutes. Rest 90 seconds. Three rounds total.
9. Between rounds, the coach adjusts the cone sea slightly — move 4–5 cones. The layout is never identical.

**Coaching observation notes (process, not scoreboard):** during each round, the coach privately counts: head-up scanning moments, clean gate exits (ball through without hitting gate cones), and foot-switch instances. These observations inform what the coach praises at round end. They are not announced as a score or ranking.

## Coach guidance

**Look for:** head-up scanning during the open-sea dribbling phase (the player should be scanning for cones, gates, and listening for the call simultaneously); clean gate exits (the player routes toward the gate from sufficient distance — not a last-second scramble); foot-alternating compliance during the sea phase.

**Cues to give:** *"Head up — where's B?"* *"Both feet."* *"You heard me — go now."* *"Find your route early."* *"Don't stop dribbling when you listen."*

**Praise** (process before outcome): head-up moments, clean gate routes, quick response to the call without losing the ball. Examples: *"Good scan there."* *"You found that gate from distance — well done."* *"Both feet — yes, keep that."*

**Don't fix yet:** light cone contact in the first round. Contact with sea cones is expected during calibration; the player is learning to manage ball closeness and spatial navigation simultaneously. Intervene only if contact is persistent and shows no improvement across two rounds. Similarly, do not fix gate-exit messiness in the first 10 minutes — route selection improves as the drill embeds.

## Levels

- **Level 1 (baseline):** as described — 10m × 10m sea, 3 gates, coach calls gate name/colour, alternating-foot rule during sea phase. 1 player.

- **Level 2:** coach adds a foot instruction to the gate call: *"B — left!"* means exit Gate B with the left foot making the final contact through the gate. Player must hold gate identity and required foot simultaneously in working memory while navigating the sea. Increases cognitive load with no change to physical setup.

- **Level 3:** coach silently adds one new cone to the sea mid-round, or removes one, without announcing the change. Player must adapt to the altered landscape without breaking rhythm. Adaptive capacity becomes an explicit demand; the player learns to expect unexpected change.

- **Level 4:** 2–3 players in the sea simultaneously, each with their own ball. Players navigate around each other as moving obstacles. The perceptual scan now includes human objects, not just static cones. Communication begins to emerge naturally (avoiding collisions). Coach gives each player individual gate calls.

- **Level 5 (elite):** reduce grid to 8m × 8m. Add a fourth gate (Gate D — west side, alongside the entry point). Coach gives compound calls: *"A, then C"* — player must exit Gate A, re-enter, then find and exit Gate C in sequence, holding the two-part sequence in working memory while navigating a denser sea. Approximates the cognitive demands of midfield play: multiple instructions, short time windows, constant repositioning.

## Variations

- **Solo home / garden version:** no coach present. Player sets a 3-minute timer. Two gates only (left and right of the entry side). Player writes a self-call sequence on a card before starting (e.g. L-R-L-L-R-R-L) and places it outside the grid, visible from inside. The cognitive demand shifts from auditory-attention to memory-recall. Useful for home practice and self-directed development.

- **Tight-space version (garden, small area):** 6m × 6m grid, 10 cones, 2 gates. Ball must remain within 1 foot's reach at all times — the tighter space enforces ultra-close control. Viable when only a small surface is available.

- **Multi-player sea (5–8 players):** all players in the sea simultaneously, each with a ball. Coach calls one gate; first player through it earns one point (process point — for head-up navigation, not just speed). Creates genuine peer pressure and navigational competition without scrimmage contact.

- **Weak-foot-only:** entire drill — sea dribbling, gate approach, and exit touch — in the weak foot only. All other parameters unchanged. Concentrated weak-foot ball-mastery volume at match-tempo.

- **Discovery band simplification (5–8 year olds):** one gate only (Gate A, clearly marked with a distinctive colour). No alternating-foot rule. Coach calls "Gate!" — player finds the gate and exits. Re-enter and continue. Duration reduced to 2-minute rounds. The cognitive demand is minimal; the motor demand (close ball contact in the cone field) is the foreground. Joy is the primary output.

## Watch points and redirect cues

- **Watch for:** player slows or stops dribbling while listening for the call — the two tasks have become sequential rather than simultaneous. **Redirect:** *"In a match, the game does not pause when someone shouts. Do not pause here."*
- **Watch for:** player's head stays down throughout the sea phase — no visible scanning. **Redirect:** *"Without looking at Gate B — point to where it is right now."* (If the player cannot answer, the scan has not happened.)
- **Watch for:** player consistently routes the same path through the sea (memorised course rather than real-time navigation). **Redirect:** *"New way through. The path you just used does not exist this round."*
- **Watch for:** gate exits that are rushed and messy — ball knocked away on exit, player loses control. **Redirect:** *"You found the gate — now take the time to go through it properly. The gate waits."*
- **Watch for:** foot-alternating collapses under cognitive load — player reverts to strong foot only when navigating toward the gate. **Redirect:** *"Which foot just touched? Give the other one the next one."*

## Closing

Five-minute coach-led reflection after the final round:

- *"When was it easiest to hear my call — at the start of a round, or at the end? What do you think changed?"*
- *"What happened to your dribbling when you started navigating toward a gate — did the ball get further away from your foot?"*
- *"Where in the sea did you feel most comfortable? Where felt difficult — and why?"*
- *"If there were defenders moving in the sea instead of cones, what would you need to do differently — and what would stay the same?"*
