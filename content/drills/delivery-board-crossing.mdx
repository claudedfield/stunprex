## Introduction

Most youth crossing is a hopeful ball lofted into a crowd. Good crossing is a choice: the wide player reads the run being made and the defensive picture, and delivers the specific ball that run needs — a driven ball across the six-yard box for a near-post run, a cut-back for a runner arriving at the penalty spot, a whipped far-post ball for a back-post header (Conviction 4 — the cross is a struck contact whose quality and type decide everything, the foundation principle applied to delivery). The Delivery Board trains crossing as a menu of deliberate deliveries to target zones, not one stock cross.

It grooves the technique of each cross type through focused repetition (Conviction 27 — specificity wins; each cross is trained as the exact game delivery, to a real target zone). It trains both feet, because a winger who can only cross with one foot is forced inside and predictable (Conviction 6 — both feet, and a wide player especially needs the outside foot to deliver early). And it is measured on the *right ball to the right zone*, process over the goal it might have produced (Conviction 21). The board overloads the demand by asking for varied, accurate deliveries under a clock, so the match's cross feels unhurried (Conviction 36).

## Setup

```
        [GOAL] + target zones in the box:
        ┌───────────────────────────┐
        │  NEAR     PEN-SPOT    FAR  │   ▯ near-post zone
        │  ▯          ▯          ▯   │   ▯ penalty-spot (cut-back) zone
        └───────────────────────────┘   ▯ far-post zone
        (W) wide player delivers from the channel, byline to deep
```

- **Wide player (W)** delivers from a wide channel, from positions ranging from deep to the byline.
- **Target zones** in the box: near-post, penalty-spot (cut-back), far-post — marked with cones or a target/teammate.
- Crosses are scored on landing in the called zone with the right type of ball.

## Description

**The delivery menu (taught and grooved):**

- **Driven cross** — low and hard across the face for a near-post run.
- **Cut-back** — pulled back to the penalty spot from the byline for an arriving runner.
- **Whipped far-post** — curled away from the keeper to the back post.

**One rep:**

1. W receives or carries into the wide channel and reads the called target zone (or the run being made) (Conviction 21 — the read of which ball is needed is the process being measured).
2. W delivers the **matching cross type** to that zone, off whichever foot the position demands (Conviction 4, Conviction 6).
3. The delivery is scored on landing in the zone with the right ball — a driven ball to the near zone, a cut-back to the spot, a whip to the far post.
4. Alternate the foot and the cross type; deliver from deep and from the byline (different crosses).

The measure is **the right ball, accurately, to the called zone, off both feet** — not power or how spectacular the cross looks.

## Progressions

- **Level 1 (baseline):** static ball, deliver each cross type to its zone with no time pressure; groove the technique (Conviction 27).
- **Level 2 (off the carry):** W carries into the channel before delivering, so the cross comes off movement.
- **Level 3 (called zone, late):** the target zone is called late; W must read it and deliver the matching ball quickly (Conviction 21).
- **Level 4 (both feet mandated):** the position forces the foot — byline cut-backs off the inside foot, early balls off the outside — so both feet deliver (Conviction 6).
- **Level 5 (elite — under pressure, to a live run):** a defender pressures the delivery and a real runner attacks the called zone; W must beat the block and deliver the right ball to the run at speed (Conviction 36).

## Coach guidance

**Look for:**

- **The right ball for the zone.** A driven ball to the near post, a cut-back to the spot, a whip to the far post — does the cross *type* match the target, or is it one stock cross every time? (Conviction 4.)
- **Accuracy to the zone.** Does the ball land where the runner is, or sail into no-man's-land? Accuracy beats power.
- **Both feet.** Can W deliver off the foot the position demands, or do they always shape onto the strong one and telegraph it? (Conviction 6.)

**Cues:** *"What run is being made? Deliver the ball it needs."* · *"Driven to the near post, cut-back to the spot, whip to the far."* · *"Accuracy first — power second."* · *"Which foot does this position want?"*

**Praise:** the matched, accurate delivery off both feet. *"You read the near-post run and drove it across — right ball, right zone. That's a delivery, not a hope."* (Conviction 21, Conviction 27.)

**Don't fix yet:** the power of the cross in early sessions — first build accuracy and the right ball type; the pace on the delivery follows the technique.

## Watch points

- **One stock cross every time,** regardless of the run. *"Same ball every time — but the runs are different. What does this one need?"* (Conviction 4.)
- **The ball sails over everyone** into no-man's-land. *"Accuracy to the zone. Where's the runner — put it there."*
- **W always shapes onto the strong foot,** delaying and telegraphing. *"That position wanted your other foot for an early ball. Use it."* (Conviction 6.)
- **The cut-back is lofted** instead of pulled back low. *"From the byline it's a low cut-back, not a chip — pull it to the spot."* (Conviction 27.)

## Closing reflection

- *"Which cross type is most reliable for you, and which needs work?"*
- *"How do you decide which ball to deliver?"*
- *"Which foot is weaker for crossing, and how does that limit you?"*
